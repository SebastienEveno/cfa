## Summary: Ownership Structures & Corporate Governance (CFA Level II)

---

### Dispersed vs. Concentrated Ownership

| Type | Definition | Key Examples |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Dispersed** | Many shareholders, none with individual control | US, UK, Australia, Ireland |
| **Concentrated** | One shareholder or group controls the company | Most of world (38/47 OECD jurisdictions) |
| **Hybrid** | Mix of both | Canada, Germany, Japan, Netherlands, Switzerland |

> **Key fact**: Concentrated ownership is the **global norm** — dispersed ownership is the exception, primarily found in Anglo-Saxon markets.

---

### How Control Can Exceed Ownership — Key Mechanisms

| Mechanism | How It Works |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Horizontal ownership** | Cross-shareholdings between companies with mutual business interests |
| **Vertical/pyramid ownership** | Controlling interest in holding companies, which in turn control operating companies |
| **Dual-class shares** | One share class has superior/sole voting rights; other class has inferior/none |
| **Voting caps** | Legal restrictions limiting voting rights of large positions (used by some governments to deter foreign control) |

---

### The Two Core Conflicts

| Conflict | Structure | Who Wins | Who Loses |
|---------|-----------|---------|----------|
| **Principal–Agent** | Dispersed ownership + dispersed voting | Strong managers | Weak shareholders |
| **Principal–Principal** | Concentrated ownership + concentrated voting | Strong controlling shareholders | Minority shareholders |

**Principal–Agent**: Managers may pursue self-interest at shareholders' expense — weak shareholders can't monitor or discipline them.

**Principal–Principal**: Controlling shareholders may extract private benefits (related-party transactions, resource allocation) at minority shareholders' expense.

---

### Types of Influential Shareholders

| Shareholder Type | Key Characteristics | Main Risk |
|----------------|--------------------|-----------| 
| **Banks** | Common in Europe & Asia; often both lender and equity holder | May push company to take unfavorable loans → conflict of interest |
| **Families** | Dominant in Latin America, Asia, Europe; often interlocking directorates | Poor transparency, weak minority rights, management quality |
| **State (SOEs)** | Strategically important sectors; mixed-ownership model | Social/policy goals may override shareholder value |
| **Institutional investors** | Mutual funds, pension funds, insurers, hedge funds | Generally positive — promote accountability; but rarely controlling in dispersed markets |
| **Group companies** | Horizontal/vertical cross-holdings (e.g. Samsung, Grupo Carso) | Related-party transactions harm minority shareholders |
| **Private equity** | Venture capital / LBO firms; majority control | Generally positive — improve governance, performance-based pay |
| **Foreign investors** | Particularly influential in emerging markets | Can demand higher transparency; may benefit local minority shareholders |
| **Insiders (managers/directors)** | Dual role as shareholder and decision-maker | Alignment improves with ownership; but large positions → entrenchment risk |

### Effects on Corporate Governance Practices

#### Board Independence
- Higher in **dispersed** ownership markets (US requires independent audit, nomination, compensation committees)
- Lower in **concentrated** markets — principal–agent problem less severe, so less need for independent monitoring
- Most OECD countries require or recommend 20%–50%+ independent directors

#### Board Structures

| Structure | Composition | Common Where |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| **One-tier** | Single board of executive + non-executive directors | Most common globally |
| **Two-tier** | Supervisory board + management board | Germany, Argentina, Russia (mandatory); Brazil, France (optional) |

Two-tier supervisory board functions: reviews books/records, annual reports, auditors, management compensation.

#### Special Voting Arrangements
- Several jurisdictions (Brazil, India, UK, Italy, Israel, Turkey, Portugal) have **minority shareholder protections** in board nomination/election
- UK premium listing: independent directors must be approved by **both** all shareholders *and* non-controlling shareholders separately when a controlling shareholder exists

#### Corporate Governance Codes
- Most countries use **"comply or explain"** approach — disclose adoption or justify non-compliance
- Some go further: Japan requires companies with no outside directors to **justify** the absence
- Others use company law (Chile) or stock exchange listing rules (India) instead of national codes

#### Stewardship Codes
- Voluntary codes encouraging **institutional investors** to actively exercise shareholder rights and monitor companies
- UK Stewardship Code: semi-mandatory — UK asset managers must publish a "comply or explain" statement

### Master Summary Table

| Ownership + Voting | Shareholder Power | Manager Power | Primary Conflict |
|-------------------|-----------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Dispersed + Dispersed | Weak | Strong | Principal–Agent |
| Concentrated + Concentrated | Strong | Weak | Principal–Principal |
| Dispersed + Concentrated (dual class/pyramid) | Weak (minority) | Weak | Principal–Principal |
| Concentrated + Dispersed (voting caps) | Limited (foreign) | Variable | Regulatory/political |

### Exam Tips

- Know the **dispersed vs. concentrated** distinction cold — it drives everything else
- **Principal–agent** = manager problem (dispersed markets); **principal–principal** = minority shareholder problem (concentrated markets)
- Dual-class shares and pyramid structures are the key mechanisms that **disconnect ownership from control**
- Independent directors matter **more** in dispersed markets; **less critical** in concentrated markets where controlling shareholders already monitor management
- SOEs: governance risk = **public policy goals may conflict with profit maximization**
