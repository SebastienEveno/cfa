## Summary: Valuation Equivalence of Cash Dividends and Share Repurchases (CFA Level II)

---

### Core Principle

> Under equal tax treatment and equal information content, a share repurchase and a cash dividend of the same amount are **equivalent in terms of shareholder wealth**.

The *method* of distributing cash does not, by itself, create or destroy value.

---

### The Baseline Equivalence (Example 16 Logic)

**Setup:** 10M shares at Rs200/share. Rs500M to distribute.

| | Cash Dividend | Share Repurchase |
|--|--------------|-----------------|
| Amount distributed | Rs50/share (Rs500M ÷ 10M) | Rs500M buys 2.5M shares at Rs200 |
| Share price after | Rs200 − Rs50 = **Rs150** | Still **Rs200** (fewer shares, same total value) |
| Shares held | 10M (unchanged) | 7.5M remaining |
| Shareholder wealth | Rs150 (stock) + Rs50 (dividend) = **Rs200** | Rs200/share × same % ownership = **Rs200** |

**Result**: Wealth is identical either way — the distribution method is irrelevant under these assumptions.

---

### When Equivalence Breaks Down

The "all else equal" assumption requires:
- **Same tax treatment** for dividends and capital gains
- **Same information content** (no differential signaling)

When either condition fails, the two methods are **no longer equivalent** and one may be preferred over the other (e.g. tax-advantaged buybacks, or stronger dividend signaling).

---

### The Wealth Transfer Problem — Direct Negotiation at a Premium (Example 17 Logic)

If a company repurchases shares from **one shareholder at a premium** to market price, wealth is **transferred away from remaining shareholders**.

**Setup:** 10M shares at ZAR200. Company offers to buy ZAR500M from one shareholder at **ZAR250** (ZAR50 premium).

- Shares repurchased: 500M ÷ 250 = **2M shares** (vs. 2.5M shares at fair market price)
- Company pays ZAR50 premium × 2M shares = **ZAR100M excess** paid to one shareholder
- Remaining shareholders effectively **subsidize** that premium

> This is the economic rationale behind why **greenmail transactions** (buying out hostile shareholders at a premium) harm remaining shareholders — they are a direct wealth transfer, not a neutral distribution.

---

### Key Takeaways for the Exam

| Scenario | Effect on Shareholder Wealth |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Buyback at market price vs. cash dividend (equal tax/info) | **Equivalent** — no wealth created or destroyed |
| Buyback financed with fairly priced debt | Neutral if earnings yield = after-tax debt cost |
| Buyback at **premium** to market (direct negotiation) | **Remaining shareholders lose** — wealth transferred to selling shareholder |
| Tax disadvantage of dividends vs. buybacks | Buyback **preferred** — higher after-tax wealth |
| Differential signaling | Whichever sends the **stronger positive signal** may be preferred |
