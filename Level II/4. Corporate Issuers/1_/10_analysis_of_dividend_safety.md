## Summary: Analysis of Dividend Safety (CFA Level II)

---

### Why This Matters

The 2008–2009 recession produced the **largest wave of dividend cuts since the Great Depression** — S&P 500 dividends fell 25%, UK companies cut 15%, Australia 9%. Analysts need tools to anticipate cuts *before* they happen.

---

### Two Core Measures of Dividend Safety

#### 1. Earnings-Based Ratios (Traditional)

$$\text{Dividend payout ratio} = \frac{\text{Dividends}}{\text{Net income}}$$

$$\text{Dividend coverage ratio} = \frac{\text{Net income}}{\text{Dividends}}$$

**Interpretation benchmarks:**

| Coverage Ratio | Payout Ratio | Signal |
|---------------|-------------|--------|
| 1.7x – 2.5x | 40% – 60% | Normal range for large mature companies |
| Declining trend | Rising trend | Increasing risk of cut |
| ~1.0x | ~100% | Dividend **in jeopardy** |
| <1.0x | >100% | Unsustainable — cut very likely |

> **Qualitative adjustments**: A coverage ratio at 1.0x may still be acceptable if the earnings dip is clearly temporary (strike, natural disaster). Stable/rising dividend history = positive; past cuts = negative mark.

---

#### 2. FCFE-Based Ratios (Superior — Cash Flow Perspective)

**Why FCFE matters**: Net income is an accounting measure — it can be positive while actual cash available for dividends is negative. FCFE represents *true* distributable cash.

$$\text{FCFE} = \text{CFO} - \text{Capital expenditures} + \text{Net borrowing}$$

$$\text{FCFE coverage ratio} = \frac{\text{FCFE}}{\text{Dividends} + \text{Share repurchases}}$$

**Interpretation:**

| FCFE Coverage | Meaning |
|--------------|---------|
| **> 1.0x** | Company distributing less than it generates — building liquidity |
| **= 1.0x** | Returning all available cash to shareholders |
| **< 1.0x** | Paying out **more than it can afford** — unsustainable; will need to cut, issue equity, or reduce capex |

> **Note**: Repurchases are included because they are also a cash outflow to shareholders — analyzing dividends alone understates total distribution pressure.

---

### Red Flags for Dividend Safety

Analysts should be **especially alert** when a company sustains distributions by:

| Warning Sign | Why It's Dangerous |
|-------------|-------------------|
| **Cutting productive capex** | Erodes future earnings power |
| **Adding net debt** to fund dividends | Increases financial risk; restricted in downturns |
| **FCFE coverage falling below 1.0x** | Mathematically unsustainable |
| **Earnings coverage approaching 1.0x** | Small earnings decline = dividend at risk |
| **Abnormally high dividend yield** | Market is pricing in an expected cut |
| **High payout vs. peers** | Relative warning signal |
| **High leverage** | Limits access to debt financing in downturns |

---

### The High Yield Trap — Market Signal

An **unusually high dividend yield** is often the market's way of signaling an expected cut:

- Investors sell the stock → price falls → yield rises mechanically
- The yield looks attractive but is a **warning, not an opportunity**

**Evidence:**
- Research (2005–2015 S&P 500): top 5% yielding stocks were **overrepresented 8%+ in the bottom performance decile**
- Madden study: 78.6% of high-dividend-universe companies had questionable ability to sustain dividends in the early recession vs. only 30.7% of the broader index

> **Exam tip**: A very high yield relative to a company's history and forward earnings is a **red flag**, not a buy signal.

### Lygon Resources — Worked Case Study

A deteriorating company perfectly illustrating how both ratios signal a coming cut:

| Year | Payout Ratio | Earnings Coverage | FCFE | FCFE Coverage |
|------|-------------|-----------------|------|--------------|
| 2015 | 22.4% | 4.46x | A$163M | 1.35x |
| 2016 | 55.9% | 1.79x | A$368M | 1.02x |
| 2017 | 69.4% | 1.44x | A$462M | **0.83x** ⚠️ |
| 2018 | **94.7%** | **1.06x** ⚠️ | A$294M | **0.91x** ⚠️ |

**Key lessons from Lygon:**
- Earnings coverage deteriorated from 4.46x to 1.06x in just 4 years — an analyst watching the trend should have flagged risk early
- FCFE coverage dropped **below 1.0x in 2017** — the repurchase program should have been stopped then (and was)
- By 2018, even with no repurchases, FCFE coverage of dividends alone was still below 1.0x → **dividend cut warranted**
- The company was sustaining distributions partly by **reducing capex** — itself a warning sign

### Master Formula Summary

$$\boxed{\text{FCFE} = \text{CFO} - \text{CapEx} + \text{Net borrowing}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{FCFE coverage} = \frac{\text{FCFE}}{\text{Dividends} + \text{Share repurchases}}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Earnings coverage} = \frac{\text{Net income}}{\text{Dividends}}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Payout ratio} = \frac{\text{Dividends}}{\text{Net income}}}$$
