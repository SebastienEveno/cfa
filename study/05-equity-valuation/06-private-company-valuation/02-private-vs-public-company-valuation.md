## Summary: Public vs. Private Company Valuation (CFA Level II)

---

### Public Company Valuation — Standard Framework

- Based on **standard issuer disclosures** and **observable market price**
- Uses **audited financial statements** to project future cash flows
- Analyst takes perspective of **outside, non-controlling investor**
- Compare intrinsic value to market price → over/undervalued conclusion

---

### Private Company Features — Two Categories

#### Company-Specific Factors

| Factor | Detail | Impact on Valuation |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| **Life-cycle stage** | May be early-stage, mature, or in liquidation | Affects growth assumptions and risk |
| **Smaller size** | Fewer business lines, customers, limited capital access | ↑ Required return (higher income variability and risk) |
| **Owner/manager overlap** | Controlling shareholders often ARE management | ↓ Principal-agent problem; but adds key-person risk |
| **Longer-term orientation** | No public shareholder pressure for short-term returns | May support better strategic decisions |
| **Family dynamics** | Family ownership dominant in many economies | Complex governance; succession issues |

#### Stock-Specific Factors (Generally Negative for Valuation)

| Factor | Detail | Impact on Valuation |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| **Illiquidity** | Limited buyers; shares cannot easily be sold | **Primary negative** → illiquidity discount |
| **Concentrated ownership** | Few shareholders, often one controlling investor | ↓ Value for minority shareholders |
| **Concentrated control** | Controlling shareholders may benefit at minority's expense | ↑ Risk of value transfer |
| **Sale restrictions** | Shareholder agreements limiting share transfers | ↓ Marketability → discount |

---

### Key Conceptual Points

**Principal-agent problem**: Much **reduced** in private companies where owners = managers.

**Private equity strategy**: Acquire underperforming public companies → restructure under private control → sell at higher price (to another buyer or via IPO).

**Wider spectrum**: Private companies have **greater dispersion of risk and return** than public companies — valuation assumptions diverge more due to:
- Purpose of valuation
- Analyst's perspective
- Quality/quantity of financial information available

---

### The Mittelstand — Family Firm Example

- German/Austrian/Swiss small & medium enterprises
- >90% of German companies, 58% of workforce, >1/3 of domestic sales
- Globally competitive niche manufacturers (capital goods, electronics)
- Family ownership = typical private company model in both developed AND developing markets

---

### Knowledge Check Answers

**Q1 (Thunder Corp — least likely source of difference):**
→ **B. Principal-agent issues** — Thunder's managers are separate from owners (similar to public companies), so this is NOT a distinctive difference from public companies.

**Q2 (Sun and Moon — different per-share values across stakes):**
→ **C. Concentrated control** — The two 35% partners have control; the 10% partners do not. Control vs. minority status creates different per-share values (control premium vs. lack of control discount).

**Q3 (Privacy Group vs. PT Corp — key difference):**
→ **B. Illiquidity of shares** — Both firms are family-owned with owner/manager overlap and concentrated control. The only meaningful difference is that PT Corp. has **publicly traded stock** (liquid) while Privacy Group does not.

---

### Exam Tips

- **Illiquidity** = primary stock-specific negative for private company valuation
- **Owner = manager** → reduces principal-agent problem (unlike most public companies)
- **Minority vs. controlling stake** → different values per share for the same company
- **Company-specific factors can be positive OR negative** (e.g., high growth potential vs. competitive disadvantage from small size)
- **Stock-specific factors are generally negative** (illiquidity, concentration, sale restrictions)
- When asked what distinguishes private from public valuation, think: **no market price, illiquidity, concentrated control, less financial disclosure**
