## Summary: Applications of Equity Valuation (CFA Level II)

---

### Eight Key Applications

| Application | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Stock selection** | Primary use — assess whether a stock is fairly priced, overpriced, or underpriced vs. intrinsic value and comparables |
| **Inferring market expectations** | Reverse-engineer what fundamentals (e.g. growth rate) are implied by the current market price |
| **Evaluating corporate events** | Assess valuation impact of M&A, divestitures, spin-offs, going-private transactions; also judge whether acquirer's stock used as currency is fairly valued |
| **Fairness opinions** | Third-party (e.g. investment bank) opinion on whether merger terms are fair — valuation is central |
| **Evaluating business strategies** | Companies assess which strategy maximizes shareholder value |
| **Communicating with analysts/shareholders** | Common language for management-investor-analyst discussions on value-affecting decisions |
| **Appraising private businesses** | Valuations for acquisitions, buy-sell agreements, estate taxes — no market price available |
| **Share-based compensation** | Estimating fair value of restricted stock grants and stock options |

---

### Inferring Market Expectations — The Key Technique

**Process (reverse engineering):**
1. Select an appropriate valuation model linking value to fundamentals
2. Input all known/estimated fundamentals **except** the one of interest
3. Solve for the value of that fundamental that makes model value = current market price
4. **Judge whether that implied fundamental is reasonable**

**Why it matters:**
- Analyst can compare implied expectations to own forecasts → identify mispricing
- Implied fundamental from one company can serve as a **benchmark** for comparable companies

### The Intel Case Study — Cornell (2001)

**Event**: Intel's Q3 2000 revenue growth announcement fell 2–7% below prior guidance/analyst estimates → stock fell **~30% in 5 days** ($61.50 → $43.31)

**Cornell's reverse-engineering analysis:**

| Price | Implied Growth Rate |
|-------|-------------------|
| $61.50 (pre-announcement) | **20%/year for 10 years**, then 6% |
| $43.31 (post-announcement) | **<15%/year for 10 years** |

**Revenue difference by 2009**: $50 billion lower under the revised implied growth rate

**Problem**: The announcement only cited a **cyclical slowdown in Europe** — no fundamental long-run change. Yet the price drop implied a **permanently lower long-run growth rate**.

**Two interpretations:**

| Interpretation | Explanation |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Market overreaction** | Investors irrationally extrapolated a short-term miss into permanent impairment |
| **Rational correction** | Stock was already overvalued at $61.50; announcement acted as a **catalyst** to correct a pre-existing mispricing |

**Cornell's conclusion**: The 20% implied pre-announcement growth was **far above** Intel's actual 5-year historical growth rate → stock was likely **overvalued** before the announcement.

---

### Key Exam Takeaways

- **Inferring expectations** is both a **valuation tool** (find implied growth) and an **analytical framework** (judge reasonableness vs. history/peers)
- Large single-day price moves can reflect either **market overreaction** or **rational correction of prior overvaluation** — distinguishing between them requires valuation work
- A **catalyst** can cause convergence between market price and intrinsic value even if the catalyst itself doesn't contain enough new fundamental information to justify the full price move
- **Stock selection** is the primary application — all other applications flow from the same valuation toolkit
- For active management to succeed: analyst's expectations must differ from **consensus** AND be **more correct on average**
