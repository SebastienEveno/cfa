## Summary: Issues in Model Selection and Interpretation (CFA Level II)

---

### Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP) Valuation

**Definition**: Value a company as the **sum of its individual business segments**, each valued as an independent going-concern entity.

**Also called**: Breakup value or private market value.

**When most useful:**
- Company operates in **multiple industries** with different valuation characteristics
- Evaluating value that could be unlocked through **spin-offs, split-offs, tracking stocks, or IPO carve-outs**
- Identifying whether a **conglomerate discount** exists

**Process:**
1. Value each segment separately using appropriate multiples/models for that industry
2. Sum segment values
3. Adjust for **corporate/unallocated items** (overhead, taxes, assets/liabilities not attributable to segments)

$$\text{SOTP Value} = \sum_{i} \text{Segment}_i \text{ Value} \pm \text{Corporate adjustments}$$

---

### Conglomerate Discount

**Definition**: The market applies a **discount to multi-business conglomerates** vs. focused single-business companies.

**Three explanations:**

| Explanation | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Inefficient internal capital markets** | Capital allocation across divisions doesn't maximize shareholder value |
| **Endogenous factors** | Poorly performing companies tend to diversify via unrelated acquisitions — discount reflects underlying poor performance |
| **Measurement error** | Conglomerate discounts may not truly exist — evidence may be flawed |

**Most observable when**: Companies divest low-synergy segments → SOTP > current market value → **strategic action catalyst** (spin-off, divestiture).

> If breakup value **exceeds** going-concern value → management should consider restructuring.

### Three Criteria for Model Selection

| Criterion | Key Question |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Consistent with company characteristics** | Does the model suit the company's business model and asset nature? |
| **Data availability and quality** | Are reliable inputs available for the chosen model? |
| **Purpose and analyst perspective** | What is the valuation for, and what perspective is being taken? |

#### Criterion 1: Company Characteristics

| Company Type | Preferred Model |
|-------------|----------------|
| Bank (mostly marketable assets) | Asset-based / relative valuation on book value |
| Mature dividend-paying company | DDM |
| Growth company (no dividends) | FCF models |
| Natural resource company | Asset-based (value of reserves) |
| Negative/volatile earnings company | EV/EBITDA or P/Sales multiples (avoid P/E) |

#### Criterion 2: Data Availability

| Situation | Implication |
|-----------|------------|
| Company never paid dividends + no dividend policy info | DDM not reliable → use FCFE/FCFF |
| Highly volatile or negative earnings | P/E comparisons meaningless → use EV/EBITDA or P/Sales |
| Few comparable companies | Relative valuation difficult → rely more on absolute models |

#### Criterion 3: Purpose and Perspective

| Perspective | Preferred Approach |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Minority investor** | DDM (dividends = actual cash received) |
| **Control investor / acquirer** | FCF models (can redirect cash flows; dividends irrelevant) |
| Reading others' research | Consider writer's **perspective and potential biases** in model choice and inputs |

### Multi-Factor Practice

**In practice, analysts use multiple models:**
- Merrill Lynch Institutional Factor Survey (2018): institutional investors use **~17 valuation factors** on average
- Multiple models provide **incremental and cross-validating insights**

**Common multi-factor applications:**
- **Stock screens**: filter by multiple criteria simultaneously
- **Factor rankings**: rank securities on each factor, combine into composite rank with assigned weights
- **Quantitative models**: assign weights to individual factors systematically

---

### Exam Tips

- **SOTP > current EV** = conglomerate discount present → potential catalyst for spin-off or divestiture
- **SOTP most useful**: multi-industry conglomerates, restructuring evaluation
- **Conglomerate discount**: three explanations — inefficient internal capital, endogenous, measurement error
- **Model selection**: no single right answer — match to company type, data quality, and purpose
- **Control acquirer → FCF models** (not DDM) — can redirect cash flows regardless of dividend policy
- **Volatile/negative earnings → avoid P/E** — use EV/EBITDA or P/Sales instead
- **Using multiple models is standard practice** — each provides complementary information; convergence of models strengthens valuation confidence
