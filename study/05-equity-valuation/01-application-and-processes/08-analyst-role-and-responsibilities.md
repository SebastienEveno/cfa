## Summary: The Analyst's Role and Responsibilities (CFA Level II)

---

### Converting Forecasts to Valuation — Two Key Steps

#### 1. Sensitivity Analysis
- Tests how changes in **key assumptions** affect the valuation output
- Common applications:
  - Growth rate changes (decomposed into sales growth + margin assumptions)
  - Discount rate changes
  - Competitive scenario analysis (e.g., price cuts vs. advertising spend vs. product changes in response to a competitor)

#### 2. Situational Adjustments

Three adjustments not typically captured in standard quantitative models:

| Adjustment | Direction | When Applied |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Control premium** | ↑ Value | Investor acquiring a controlling position (>50%, or sometimes much less) — gains control of board, asset redeployment, capital structure changes |
| **Lack of marketability discount** | ↓ Value | Non-publicly traded stocks — no public market exists |
| **Illiquidity discount / Blockage factor** | ↓ Value | Publicly traded stocks with thin markets, or large block sales relative to trading volume |

> **Blockage factor**: Selling a large block of shares → realized price lower than market price for a smaller position.

### Types of Analysts

| Analyst Type | Employer | Primary Output | Audience |
|-------------|---------|---------------|---------|
| **Sell-side** | Brokerage firms | Research reports + buy/sell recommendations | Retail and institutional brokerage clients |
| **Buy-side** | Investment management firms, bank trust departments | Valuation judgments to portfolio managers or investment committees | Internal investment decision makers |
| **Quantitative** | Investment firms | Security ranking models; factor-based selection | Quantitative portfolio strategies |
| **Corporate** | Corporations | Pension asset management; M&A target valuation | Internal management/boards |
| **Independent** | Financial information vendors | Research reports and corporate analysis | Public/subscribers |

---

### What Analysts Are Expected to Do

From the AIMR Senate testimony (post-Enron, 2002):

1. **Research** assigned companies and industries thoroughly
2. **Forecast** future prospects
3. **Determine fair value** using appropriate valuation models
4. **Compare** fair value to market price → generate investment recommendation
5. **Exercise due diligence**: reading filings, talking to management (beyond conference calls), competitors, employees, industry contacts
6. **Understand industry dynamics** and macroeconomic conditions

> **Buy recommendation**: fair value significantly **above** current market price
> **Due diligence**: investigation and analysis supporting a recommendation — failure may create legal liability

---

### Three Roles Analysts Play in Capital Markets

| Role | Benefit |
|------|---------|
| **Helping clients** | Better buy/sell decisions → achievement of investment objectives |
| **Market efficiency** | Informed trading → prices better reflect intrinsic values → capital flows to highest-value uses |
| **Monitoring management** | Effective oversight reduces manager-shareholder conflicts (Jensen & Meckling 1976) |

---

### Professional Standards

Two dimensions of analyst accountability:

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|------------|
| **Competence** | High training, experience, and discipline (CFA exam + work experience requirements) |
| **Conduct** | Ethical behavior toward public, clients, employers, fellow analysts — governed by CFA Code of Ethics and Standards of Professional Conduct |

> Analysts are in a **position of trust** — both technical skill and ethical conduct are required. The Code and Standards guide analysts toward **independent, well-researched, and well-documented** analysis.

---

### Exam Tips

- **Control premium**: applies when acquiring a **controlling stake** — not just >50%; even a smaller % can confer significant influence
- **Illiquidity discount** ≠ **lack of marketability discount**: illiquidity applies to thinly traded **public** stocks; lack of marketability applies to **private/non-traded** securities
- **Blockage factor**: large block sale → price below market → relevant for large institutional positions
- **Sell-side** = brokerage (distribute research widely); **buy-side** = investment management (research is internal input)
- **Sensitivity analysis is mandatory** — single-point estimates are insufficient; always test key assumptions
- Analysts have **three constituencies**: clients, markets, and capital suppliers (shareholders)
