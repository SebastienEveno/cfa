## Summary: Understanding the Business (CFA Level II)

---

### The Five-Step Valuation Process

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Understand the business** | Industry/competitive analysis + financial statement analysis |
| **2. Forecast company performance** | Pro forma sales, earnings, dividends, financial position |
| **3. Select valuation model** | Based on company characteristics and context |
| **4. Convert forecasts to valuation** | Mechanical output + judgment |
| **5. Apply conclusions** | Investment recommendation, transaction opinion, strategic evaluation |

---

### Step 1: Understanding the Business — Three Core Questions

#### Question 1: How attractive is the industry?

Use **Porter's Five Forces** to assess inherent industry profitability:

| Force | Favorable Conditions (Higher Profitability) |
|-------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Rivalry** | Few competitors, strong brand differentiation |
| **Threat of new entrants** | High entry barriers (capital, regulation, scale) |
| **Threat of substitutes** | Few substitutes or high switching costs |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | Many suppliers → company has leverage |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | Many customers → company has leverage |

Also consider: demographic trends, technological shifts, regulatory changes — anything affecting **long-term profitability and growth**.

---

#### Question 2: What is the company's competitive position and strategy?

**Key indicator**: Level and trend of **market share** → shows relative competitive position

**Porter's Generic Strategies:**

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Cost leadership** | Lowest-cost producer at industry-average prices | Budget airlines (single aircraft type, single class) |
| **Differentiation** | Unique products/services commanding premium prices | Luxury goods, patented pharmaceuticals |
| **Focus** | Cost or differentiation advantage within a **niche segment** | Boutique investment banks |

**Business model** = how the company makes money: which customers, what products/services, how delivered and financed.

---

#### Question 3: How well has the company executed its strategy?

**Analytical approach:**
- Review financial reports **across multiple time periods** — not just the most recent
- Look at annual reports from 10 years, 5 years, and 2 years prior → reveals how management has historically anticipated challenges and adapted

**Two important caveats:**

| Caveat | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Qualitative factors matter** | Ownership structure, intellectual property, license terms, legal contingencies — non-numeric but value-relevant |
| **Avoid simple extrapolation** | Past performance ≠ future performance; economic forces drive **regression toward the mean** |

**Regression toward the mean in corporate performance:**
- Successful companies → attract new competitors → margins compress
- Poorly performing companies → restructuring → profitability often improves
- **Implication for long-term forecasts**: beyond 10 years, analysts typically assume **convergence to the economy's average growth rate**

---

### Exam Tips

- **Porter's Five Forces**: know which conditions make each force **favorable vs. unfavorable** for industry profitability
- **Three generic strategies**: cost leadership, differentiation, focus — know the distinctions and real-world examples
- **Industry analysis purpose**: not to produce a formal framework, but to identify the **most important economic drivers** and areas requiring sensitivity analysis
- **Regression to the mean**: above-average profitability is eroded by competition; below-average profitability is improved by restructuring → assume long-run convergence to economy-wide growth
- **Historical annual reports** from multiple years reveal management's **adaptability and strategic foresight** — more valuable than just reviewing the current year's 10-year summary table
