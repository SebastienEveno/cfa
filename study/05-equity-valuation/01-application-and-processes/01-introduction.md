## Summary: Equity Valuation — Introduction (CFA Level II)

---

### What Is Valuation?

**Valuation** = estimation of an asset's value based on:
- Variables related to future investment returns
- Comparisons with similar assets
- Estimates of immediate liquidation proceeds (when relevant)

---

### Definitions of Value

| Concept | Definition | When Used |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Intrinsic value** | "True" value given complete understanding of investment characteristics | Public equity valuation — primary concept |
| **Going-concern value** | Value assuming company continues operations indefinitely | Standard assumption for most companies |
| **Liquidation value** | Value if company dissolved and assets sold individually | Financial distress; may exceed going-concern if company persistently unprofitable |
| **Fair market value** | Price between willing, informed, uncompelled buyer and seller | Private business transactions, tax assessments |
| **Fair value** (IFRS/GAAP) | Exchange price in arm's-length transaction between knowledgeable willing parties | Financial reporting / impairment testing |
| **Investment value** | Value to a **specific** buyer including synergies and buyer-specific expectations | M&A; strategic acquisitions |

> **For public equity valuation**: **intrinsic value under going-concern assumption** is the relevant concept.

---

### The Mispricing Framework

$$\boxed{V^E - P = (V - P) + (V^E - V)}$$

| Component | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| $V^E - P$ | **Perceived mispricing** (estimated value vs. market price) |
| $V - P$ | **True mispricing** (intrinsic value vs. market price) → source of alpha |
| $V^E - V$ | **Estimation error** (analyst's estimate vs. true intrinsic value) |

**Implication**: Even if true mispricing exists, analyst errors in estimating intrinsic value can cause the strategy to fail. Success requires:
1. Accurate forecasts (better than consensus)
2. Appropriate valuation model
3. **Convergence within the investment horizon** (not guaranteed)

---

### The Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox

If market prices **perfectly** reflected intrinsic value:
- Rational investors wouldn't pay to gather/analyze information
- But if no one analyzes information → prices **can't** reflect intrinsic value

**Resolution**: Investors must expect **compensation** (excess returns) for information-gathering costs → market can never be perfectly efficient → **mispricing can exist**.

---

### Going Concern vs. Liquidation

| | Going Concern | Liquidation |
|--|--------------|-------------|
| Assumption | Company continues operations | Company dissolved; assets sold |
| Typically higher when | Company adds value through operations + management | Company is persistently unprofitable |
| Liquidation timing matters | — | Orderly liquidation > forced liquidation |

---

### Key Takeaways for the Exam

- **Active managers** seek **alpha** = positive excess risk-adjusted return from identifying mispricing
- **Catalyst**: even with correct mispricing identification, a market event may be needed to close the gap
- Price may **never converge** to intrinsic value within the investor's horizon → timing risk
- **Intrinsic value** ≠ market price — this distinction is the foundation of active management
- For public equities: focus on **intrinsic value + going-concern assumption**
- **Investment value** > fair market value when buyer-specific synergies exist
