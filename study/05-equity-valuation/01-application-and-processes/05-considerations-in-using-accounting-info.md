## Summary: Considerations in Using Accounting Information (CFA Level II)

---

### Quality of Earnings — Core Concept

**Quality of earnings analysis** = scrutiny of all financial statements to assess:
1. **Sustainability** of reported performance (will it recur?)
2. **Accuracy** of reported results as reflections of economic reality

**Key principle**: Cash earnings > accrual earnings in terms of persistence.

$$\text{Accrual component} = \text{Net income} - \text{Cash component (operating + investing CF)}$$

> **Sloan (1996)**: Higher current accruals → lower future ROA. Cash component is more predictive of future net income than the accrual component. **Higher accruals = lower earnings quality.**

---

### Quality of Earnings Indicators

| Category | Red Flag | Concern |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Revenue** | Bill-and-hold sales; early revenue recognition | Accelerates income; masks declining operations |
| **Revenue** | Nonoperating gains classified as operating | Non-recurring; distorts core profitability |
| **Expenses** | Over/under-reserving (restructuring, bad debts, deferred tax) | Shifts income between periods |
| **Expenses** | Capitalizing costs that should be expensed (customer acquisition, R&D) | Defers expenses; inflates current earnings |
| **Estimates** | Long depreciable lives, low pension discount rate, high expected return on pension assets | Understates expenses; inflates earnings |
| **Balance sheet** | Off-balance sheet financing | Assets/liabilities understated |
| **Cash flow** | Bank overdrafts classified as operating CF | Inflates operating cash flow |

**General rule**: Asset account growing **much faster than sales** → possible aggressive accounting signal.

---

### Key Warning Signs in Practice

#### Pension Return Assumption (Buffett Example)
- Companies using aggressive pension return assumptions (e.g., 8%) → **understate pension expense → overstate net income**
- Information found in: **footnotes to financial statements**
- Buffett's point: To achieve 8% overall return with 28% in bonds at 5%, equities must earn 9.2% after fees — historically implausible over long periods

#### Accounts Receivable Analysis (Tangoe Case)
- SEC charged with: recognizing revenue from customers unlikely to pay + understating bad debt allowance
- Financial impact: ↑ Revenue, ↑ Receivables, Net income > Operating CF
- **Early warning sign**: 
  - AR turnover ↓ (4.6× → 4.2×)
  - Days receivable ↑ (79 → 87 days)
  - Revenue grew 12%; receivables grew **32%** → red flag

#### Expense Capitalization (Livent Case)
- Capitalized pre-production costs → deferred expenses → **inflated EPS**
- Balance sheet showed inflated assets (deferred costs)
- **Critical EBITDA distortion**:
  - Reported debt/EBITDA (1996): **1.7×**
  - Adjusted (ex. amortization add-back): **5.5×**
  - 1997: Reported EBITDA = +$58.3M; Adjusted EBITDA = **−$52.6M**
- Company declared **bankruptcy in 1998**; founders convicted of fraud

> **Key lesson**: Adding back amortization of improperly capitalized costs to compute EBITDA **overstates financial strength** — analysts must understand what is being amortized before adding it back.

---

### Cash Flow vs. Net Income Check

| Signal | Concern |
|--------|---------|
| Net income >> Operating cash flow | High accruals; potential revenue manipulation |
| Receivables growing faster than revenue | Revenue recognition issues |
| Assets growing much faster than sales | Aggressive capitalization of expenses |

---

### Fraud Risk Indicators

Organized into three categories (IAASB/PCAOB framework):
- **Incentives** to commit fraud
- **Opportunity** to commit fraud  
- **Attitude** toward committing fraud

**Key red flags:**

| Red Flag | Category |
|---------|---------|
| Excessive pressure to meet revenue/earnings targets | Incentive |
| Dominant, aggressive management or individual | Opportunity/Attitude |
| Compensation tied heavily to stock price or profitability | Incentive |
| Aggressive/unrealistic earnings commitments to analysts | Incentive |
| Economic pressure (declining margins, market share loss) | Incentive |
| Pressure to meet debt covenants | Incentive |
| Related-party transactions | Opportunity |
| Complex organizational structure | Opportunity |
| High management/director/legal counsel turnover | Attitude |
| Disputes with or changes in auditors | Attitude |
| History of securities/reporting violations or late filings | Attitude |

### Key Formulas & Ratios

$$\text{AR Turnover} = \frac{\text{Revenue}}{\text{Average AR}} \quad \downarrow \text{ signals collection problems}$$

$$\text{Days Receivable} = \frac{365}{\text{AR Turnover}} \quad \uparrow \text{ signals collection problems}$$

$$\text{Accruals} = \text{Net income} - (\text{CFO} + \text{CFI})$$

$$\text{EBITDA (adjusted)} = \text{Earnings} + \text{Interest} + \text{Taxes} + \text{D\&A (economic only)}$$

---

### Exam Tips

- **Higher accruals = lower earnings quality** = lower future ROA (Sloan 1996)
- **Net income > Operating CF** = primary red flag for quality concerns
- **Pension assumptions**: high expected return on assets → understates pension expense → overstates net income → footnotes are the source
- **Livent lesson**: EBITDA add-back of amortization is only appropriate for **true** non-cash, non-economic items — improperly capitalized costs should NOT be added back
- **Tangoe lesson**: AR growing faster than revenue = early warning of revenue manipulation
- **Fraud triangle**: incentive + opportunity + attitude/rationalization
- **Asset growing >> sales**: universal red flag for aggressive accounting across all categories
