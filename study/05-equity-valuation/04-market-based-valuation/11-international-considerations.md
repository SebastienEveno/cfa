## Summary: Other Enterprise Value Multiples (CFA Level II)

---

### EV Multiple Denominator Comparison

All EV multiples add back interest to net income because these measures flow to **all capital providers** (debt + equity).

| Measure | Formula (from Net Income) | When to Use |
|---------|--------------------------|------------|
| **FCFF** | NI + Int(1-t) + D&A − WCInv − FCInv | Strongest theoretical link to valuation |
| **EBITDA** | NI + Interest + Taxes + D&A | Most widely used; capital-intensive industries |
| **EBITA** | NI + Interest + Taxes + Amortization | When amortization (intangibles) is major but depreciation is not |
| **EBIT** | NI + Interest + Taxes | When neither D&A is a major item |

> **As you move from FCFF → EBIT**: progressively **less precise** information about tax position and capital investment requirements.

### Industry-Specific EV Multiples

| Multiple | Industry |
|---------|---------|
| EV/Subscribers | Cable TV, satellite, telecom |
| EV/Reserves | Oil & gas, mining |
| EV/EBITDAR (+ rent expense) | Airlines |
| EV/Beds | Hospitals |
| EV/Users | Internet/tech platforms |

**Core concept is the same** regardless of denominator: relate **total company market value** to a **fundamental measure of company value**.

---

### EV/Sales vs. P/S

**Problem with P/S for leveraged companies**: For a debt-financed company, some sales proceeds go to debt providers — price (equity only) vs. sales (pre-financing) is a **conceptual mismatch**.

**Solution**: **EV/Sales** — includes both debt and equity in the numerator → valid comparison across different capital structures.

$$EV/S = \frac{\text{Market cap} + \text{Debt} - \text{Cash}}{\text{Net sales}}$$

**Worked example (Stora Enso):**
$$EV = 8,154 + 2,970 - 1,130 = €9,994M$$
$$EV/S = 9,994/10,486 = \mathbf{0.953\times}$$

### Sector Median Multiples (S&P 1500, FY2007 — Key Patterns)

| Sector | Trailing P/E | EV/EBITDA | Key Characteristic |
|--------|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Health Care** | 23.0× | 11.6× | High P/E reflects growth expectations |
| **Consumer Staples** | 19.5× | 10.7× | Stable earnings; high payout (23%) |
| **Info Tech** | 20.2× | 11.6× | High P/CF (45×) — capex-light, high D&A adjustments |
| **Financials** | 14.6× | 9.5× | High leverage (5.8×); highest dividend payout (42%) |
| **Utilities** | 16.7× | 9.1× | High leverage (3.5×); high payout (53%); high dividend yield (3.1%) |
| **Energy** | 14.4× | 7.7× | High profit margin (13.9%); low payout (4%); strong operating margin growth (12%) |
| **Telecom** | 19.6× | 6.7× | Low ROE (5.4%); low asset turnover |

**Key observations:**
- **High P/E sectors**: Health Care, IT, Consumer Staples — growth expectations or earnings quality premium
- **High dividend yield**: Utilities (3.1%), Financials (3.1%) — mature, income-oriented
- **High payout**: Utilities (53%), Financials (42%), Consumer Staples (23%)
- **High financial leverage**: Financials (5.8×), Utilities (3.5×) → explains why EV multiples are more appropriate for cross-sector comparison

### Exam Tips

- **EBITA**: use when **amortization** (not depreciation) is the dominant non-cash charge (e.g., acquisition-heavy companies with large intangible amortization)
- **EV/EBIT**: appropriate when **neither D&A is material** to the business being analyzed
- **EV/Sales preferred over P/S** when capital structures differ significantly
- **Non-financial EV multiples**: match the metric to industry economics (subscribers, reserves, beds, etc.)
- **Sector median multiples**: most useful for **relative sector valuation**, not individual company valuation (too aggregated)
- **FCFF has strongest theoretical link** to valuation — but EBITDA is more widely used in practice
- Know the **hierarchy**: EBITDA → EBITA → EBIT → FCFF in terms of specificity and theoretical strength
