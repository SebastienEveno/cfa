## Summary: FCFF and FCFE on a Uses-of-Free-Cash-Flow Basis (CFA Level II)

---

### Two Perspectives on Free Cash Flow

| Perspective | What It Shows |
|-------------|--------------|
| **Sources** (prior sections) | How FCFF/FCFE is *generated* — from NI, CFO, EBIT, EBITDA |
| **Uses** (this section) | How FCFF/FCFE is *deployed* — cash retention, debt payments, equity payments |

**Purpose of uses analysis**: A **consistency check** on the sources calculation, plus insight into **capital structure policy** and cash management decisions.

---

### General Alternatives for Positive FCFF

A firm can:
1. **Retain cash** (increase cash/marketable securities balances)
2. **Pay debt providers** (interest + net principal repayments)
3. **Pay equity providers** (dividends + net share repurchases)

For **negative** FCFF, a firm must:
- Draw down cash balances
- Borrow additional cash
- Issue new equity

---

### Capital Structure Implications

| Transaction Pattern | Effect on Leverage |
|---------------------|--------------------|
| New borrowing → used for dividends/buybacks | **↑ Leverage increases** |
| New equity issuance → used to repay debt | **↓ Leverage decreases** |

---

### Uses of FCFF Formula

$$\boxed{\text{Uses of FCFF} = \Delta\text{Cash} + Int(1-t) + (\text{Debt repayment} - \text{New borrowing}) + (\text{Dividends} + \text{Net repurchases})}$$

Components:
- **Δ Cash balance** (increase = use of cash)
- **Net payments to debt providers**:
  - $+ Int(1-t)$
  - $+$ (repayment − new borrowing), or $-$ (new borrowing − repayment) if net borrowing
- **Net payments to equity providers**:
  - $+$ dividends
  - $+$ (repurchases − issuances), or $-$ (issuances − repurchases) if net issuance

---

### Uses of FCFE Formula

$$\boxed{\text{Uses of FCFE} = \Delta\text{Cash} + (\text{Dividends} + \text{Net repurchases})}$$

**Simpler than FCFF** — excludes debt-related payments since FCFE is already net of debt cash flows.

---

### Worked Example — Pitts Corporation (2020)

**Given data:**
- Δ Cash = $200 − $190 = **$10**
- After-tax interest = $100 × (1−0.40) = **$60**
- Net borrowing = $50 (notes payable) + $25 (LT debt) = **$75**
- Dividends = **$160**
- Share repurchases/issuances = **$0**

#### Uses of FCFF (should equal $155)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Δ Cash | $10 |
| + After-tax interest | $60 |
| − New borrowing (net) | ($75) |
| + Dividends | $160 |
| + Net repurchases | $0 |
| **= Total Uses** | **$155** ✅ |

#### Uses of FCFE (should equal $170)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Δ Cash | $10 |
| + Dividends | $160 |
| + Net repurchases | $0 |
| **= Total Uses** | **$170** ✅ |

---

### Interpretation — What This Tells Us About Pitts Corporation

**Key observations:**
1. **CFO ($495M) > CapEx ($400M)** → operations generated more than enough to cover capital investment
2. **Company still borrowed $75M in new debt** — not needed for CapEx
3. **New debt was effectively used to fund dividends** ($160M)

**Conclusion**: Pitts Corporation is **actively managing its capital structure** — using new debt issuance to support shareholder distributions (dividends) rather than relying solely on internally generated cash flow. This **increases financial leverage**.

---

### Why Net Borrowing is "Subtracted" in Uses of FCFF

> New borrowing represents cash **coming IN** from debt providers (a source from their perspective), which **adds to** the cash available for other uses. In the uses formula, net new borrowing is **subtracted** because it's the opposite of a "payment to debt providers" — it's debt providers giving the company more capital, which then gets used elsewhere (dividends, cash buildup, etc.).

---

### Exam Tips

- **Uses = Sources** — always — this is the **consistency check**
- **Uses of FCFE is simpler** than uses of FCFF — no debt-related adjustments
- **Net new borrowing used for dividends/buybacks** → signals **deliberate releveraging**, not a cash flow shortfall
- **Compare CFO vs. CapEx** first — if CFO comfortably covers CapEx, any additional borrowing reflects **capital structure decisions**, not operational necessity
- **Increase in cash balance** is itself counted as a "use" of free cash flow (cash retention)
- This framework helps explain **why** a company borrowed/issued — useful for assessing sustainability of dividend policy and leverage trends
