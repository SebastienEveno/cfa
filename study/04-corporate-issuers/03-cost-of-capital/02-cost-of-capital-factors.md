## Summary: Cost of Capital Factors (CFA Level II)

---

### The Core Framework: Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

$$r_d = r_f + \text{Credit spread} \qquad r_e = R_f + \beta \times ERP$$

| Factor Type | Affects | Examples |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **Top-down** (external/systematic) | Risk-free rate, ERP, aggregate credit spreads | Macro, legal, tax environment |
| **Bottom-up** (company-specific) | Beta, company credit spread | Business model, assets, leverage |

---

### Top-Down External Factors

#### 1. Capital Availability
- More capital available → **lower cost of capital** (more favorable terms for issuers)
- Developed markets: liquid markets, stable currencies, strong rule of law → **lower spreads and ERP**
- Less developed markets: may rely on bank loans or **shadow banking** → higher costs

#### 2. Market Conditions
| Condition | Effect on Cost of Capital |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Low interest rates | ↓ Cost of debt and equity |
| Tight credit spreads | ↓ Cost of debt |
| Low inflation | ↓ Risk-free rate → ↓ WACC |
| Recession | ↑ Spreads and ERP → ↑ WACC |
| High exchange rate volatility | ↑ Currency risk → ↑ WACC |

#### 3. Legal & Regulatory Environment
- **Common law** countries → stronger investor protections → **lower spreads and ERP**
- **Civil law** countries → weaker protections → **higher costs**
- Highly regulated industries (utilities, banks) → regulatory policy directly impacts capital structure and pricing decisions

#### 4. Tax Jurisdiction
- Interest is tax-deductible in most jurisdictions → **higher marginal tax rate = greater tax shield = lower after-tax cost of debt**
- Higher corporate tax rate → **more attractive to use debt** in capital structure

> **Key formula**: After-tax cost of debt = $r_d \times (1 - t)$ — higher $t$ → lower effective debt cost

### Bottom-Up Company-Specific Factors

#### 1. Revenue, Earnings & Cash Flow Volatility

| Characteristic | Cost of Capital |
|---------------|----------------|
| Subscription/recurring revenue model | ↓ Lower |
| Pay-per-use / cyclical model | ↑ Higher |
| High customer concentration (top 5 = large %) | ↑ Higher |
| High operating leverage (fixed costs) | ↑ Higher |
| High financial leverage | ↑ Higher |
| High ESG risks | ↑ Higher |
| High earnings predictability | ↓ Lower |

**ESG link to cost of capital:**
- High carbon footprint + insufficient action → investors demand higher return
- Employee safety concerns → litigation risk → higher cost of capital
- Weak governance → management entrenchment → higher cost of capital
- *Alternative*: analysts can adjust future cash flow forecasts instead of the discount rate

#### 2. Asset Nature and Liquidity

| Asset Type | Cost of Capital |
|-----------|----------------|
| Tangible assets (PP&E) → can pledge as collateral | ↓ Lower cost of debt |
| Intangible assets (goodwill, patents) → cannot collateralize | ↑ Higher |
| Fungible, liquid assets (cash, marketable securities) | ↓ Lower |
| Non-fungible, illiquid, specialized assets | ↑ Higher |

> Note: Collateralized tangible assets → ↓ cost of debt but may ↑ cost of equity (creditors have prior claim in liquidation)

#### 3. Financial Strength, Profitability & Leverage

| Financial Metric | Cost of Capital |
|----------------|----------------|
| Higher profitability | ↓ Lower |
| Higher cash flow generation | ↓ Lower |
| Higher interest coverage (IC) ratio | ↓ Lower |
| Higher net debt/EBITDA | ↑ Higher |
| Higher D/E ratio | ↑ Higher |
| Tight liquidity | ↑ Higher |

#### 4. Security Features

| Feature | Benefits | Cost of Capital at Issuance |
|---------|---------|---------------------------|
| **Callable bond** | Issuer can refinance if rates fall | ↑ Higher yield demanded by investors |
| **Putable bond** | Investor can sell back if rates rise | ↓ Lower yield accepted by investors |
| **Convertible bond** | Investor can convert to equity | ↓ Lower yield accepted by investors |
| **Cumulative preferred** | Missed dividends must be repaid before common | ↓ Lower return accepted vs. non-cumulative |
| **Inferior voting/cash flow rights (common)** | Reduced shareholder rights | ↑ Higher return demanded |

> **Important caveat**: Lower initial cost can lead to higher future cost:
> - Putable bonds: issuer may need to refinance at higher rates when bonds are put back
> - Convertible bonds: equity dilution if investors convert

### Master Summary Table

| Factor | ↓ Lower Cost of Capital | ↑ Higher Cost of Capital |
|--------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| Capital availability | Abundant | Scarce |
| Interest rates/spreads | Low/tight | High/wide |
| Legal environment | Common law, strong protections | Civil law, weak protections |
| Tax rate | High (bigger interest tax shield) | Low |
| Revenue stability | Recurring, diversified | Cyclical, concentrated |
| Operating leverage | Low | High |
| Financial leverage | Low | High |
| Asset type | Tangible, liquid, fungible | Intangible, illiquid |
| ESG risk | Low | High |
| Debt features | Putable, convertible | Callable |
| Preferred equity | Cumulative | Non-cumulative |
| Common equity | Full voting/cash flow rights | Inferior rights |

---

### Exam Tips

- **Top-down** factors → affect all companies in a market/country (systematic)
- **Bottom-up** factors → company-specific, reflected in beta and credit spread
- **Callable** bonds → higher initial cost to issuer; **putable/convertible** → lower initial cost but potential future cost
- **Higher tax rate → lower after-tax cost of debt** (assuming interest is deductible and company has taxable income)
- ESG risks can be reflected either in the **discount rate** or in **cash flow adjustments** — both are valid analytical approaches
- Best conditions for debt issuance: **low rates + tight credit spreads** (expansionary environment)
