### Core Challenge

ESG data is mostly **voluntary, inconsistently reported, and difficult to compare** across companies. Analysts must assess both **materiality** and **investment horizon** before integrating ESG factors.

---

### Two Key Filters Before Analysis

#### 1. Materiality
- ESG information is **material** if it could affect a company's operations, financial performance, or security valuation
- Watch out: companies may **overemphasize positive** ESG info while **downplaying or omitting negative** material ESG risks
- Analysts must distinguish genuinely material ESG factors from noise

#### 2. Investment Horizon
- Short-term investors: long-term ESG risks may have **little near-term price impact**
- Long-term investors: must account for **future regulatory costs, capex upgrades, fines**
- Time horizon of ESG impact can shift suddenly (e.g. new regulation, oil spill, scandal)

> **Key insight**: ESG risks can move from long-term to short-term overnight — a sudden regulatory change or controversy can immediately materialize a previously distant risk.

---

### Industry Relevance — ESG Factors Are Not Universal

| Industry | Most Relevant ESG Pillar | Examples |
|----------|------------------------|---------|
| Energy / Mining | Environmental | Carbon emissions, stranded assets, climate regulation |
| Banking / Tech | Social | Data privacy, cybersecurity, customer satisfaction |
| All industries | Governance | Board structure, compensation, voting rights |

> Analysts must first determine **which ESG factors are relevant to the specific industry** before collecting data.

---

### Three Approaches to Identifying ESG Factors

| Approach | Method | Examples |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **1. Proprietary** | Analyst's own research and firm tools | Annual reports, 10-Ks, proxy filings, sustainability reports, news, industry groups |
| **2. ESG Data Providers** | Third-party vendors score and rank companies | MSCI, Sustainalytics |
| **3. Not-for-Profit Frameworks** | Standardized reporting initiatives | GRI, SASB, IIRC, 2DII |

**Key frameworks:**
- **GRI** (Global Reporting Initiative) — sector-specific sustainability topics
- **SASB** (Sustainable Accounting Standards Board) — materiality maps by sector
- **IIRC** (International Integrated Reporting Council) — standardized ESG disclosure framework
- **TCFD** (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) — climate risk scenario planning

### ESG Integration: Equity vs. Fixed Income

| | Equity Analysis | Credit/Fixed Income Analysis |
|--|----------------|----------------------------|
| **Primary goal** | Identify opportunities **and** mitigate downside | Primarily **mitigate downside** (bond redeems at par) |
| **Tools** | Forecast financial metrics, adjust discount rate, scenario/sensitivity analysis | Internal credit assessments, forecast ratios, credit spread/duration analysis |
| **Example** | ↑ hotel operating costs due to high employee turnover | Widening credit spreads from pending lawsuits |
| **Horizon sensitivity** | Both short and long term | Maturity-dependent — 10-year bond more exposed to long-term ESG risk than 1-year note |

### Green Bonds — Key Facts

**Definition:** Bonds whose proceeds are **earmarked for environmental or climate-beneficial projects**.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| First green bond | European Investment Bank, 2007 (Climate Awareness Bond) |
| Governing standard | **Green Bond Principles** (2014) — voluntary, maintained by ICMA |
| Labeling | Issuer's primary decision, in cooperation with underwriter |
| Credit quality | Same credit rating and bondholder recourse as issuer's conventional bonds |
| Valuation | **Same as conventional bonds** — only use of proceeds differs |
| Potential premium | May trade at tighter spread if demand is strong |

**Three unique risks of green bonds:**

| Risk | Description |
|------|------------|
| **Greenwashing** | Proceeds not actually used for genuine environmental benefit → investor overpays or violates mandate |
| **Liquidity risk** | Often held by buy-and-hold investors → thinner secondary market |
| **Verification risk** | Disclosure quality varies; independent reviews not always commissioned |

### Stranded Assets — Important ESG Concept

Assets that become **obsolete or economically unviable** due to:
- Regulatory/policy changes (e.g. carbon taxes, fossil fuel restrictions)
- Shifts in demand (e.g. coal vs. renewables)

**Analytical implication**: Long-maturity bonds of exposed companies (e.g. coal) are **far more affected** than short-maturity bonds — the ESG risk has more time to materialize.

### Exam Tips

- **Materiality** in ESG = could affect operations, financials, or security value — not just any ESG disclosure
- **Equity** ESG integration = upside + downside; **Credit** ESG integration = downside only
- **Green bonds** = same analysis as conventional bonds; unique risks = greenwashing + liquidity
- **SASB materiality maps** and **GRI sector topics** = tools for identifying industry-specific ESG factors
- **Stranded assets** = key long-term ESG risk for carbon-intensive industries
- Investment horizon determines **which ESG factors are relevant** — short-term holders may rationally ignore long-term ESG risks
