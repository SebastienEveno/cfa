## Summary: Evaluating ESG-Related Risks and Opportunities (CFA Level II)

---

### Core Framework

ESG integration translates qualitative ESG observations into **concrete financial statement and valuation adjustments**. The process flows through three levels:

```
Research (identify material ESG factors)
      ↓
Security Valuation (adjust financials / discount rate / spreads)
      ↓
Portfolio Construction (asset allocation, scenario analysis, risk management)
```

**Types of financial adjustments:**

| Statement | Typical ESG Adjustments |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Income statement | Revenues, COGS, operating margins, earnings |
| Cash flow statement | Capex, operating cash flow |
| Balance sheet | Impaired/stranded assets |
| Valuation (equity) | Discount rate, price multiple, terminal value |
| Valuation (credit) | Credit spread, CDS pricing |

### Three Worked Examples — Core Exam Material

---

#### Example 3: Environmental Factors — Beverage Company (Frizzle Drinks)

**Material ESG factor**: Water intensity (liters of water per liter of beverage produced)

**Why it matters**: Water is a key input; scarcity creates regulatory risk, reputational risk, and cost risk.

**Positive ESG signal**: Frizzle reduced water intensity 27% (2009–2021), outperforming peers significantly in 2021.

**Financial statement adjustment:**

| Metric | Peer Group | Frizzle (Pre-ESG) | Frizzle (Post-ESG) |
|--------|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| COGS / Revenue | 42% | 40% | **39%** (–1% for water savings) |
| Gross margin | 58% | 60% | **61%** |

**Valuation implication**: Better water management → lower costs → higher margins → **upward adjustment to equity valuation** relative to peers.

> **Key lesson**: Strong environmental management can be a **competitive advantage** — it translates directly into margin improvement.

---

#### Example 4: Social Factors — Pharmaceutical Company (Well Pharma)

**Material ESG factor**: Product quality (recalls, regulatory warning letters, fines, lawsuits)

**Negative ESG signal**: Well has the weakest product quality track record among peers — 4 major drug safety scandals in 5 years, most regulatory warning letters in peer group.

**Key social factors mapped to financial impacts:**

| Social Factor | Financial Impact |
|--------------|----------------|
| Product quality controversies | ↓ Sales / brand damage |
| Regulatory warning letters | ↑ Compliance costs |
| Product recalls | ↓ Revenue + ↑ recall costs |
| Regulatory fines | Non-operating provisions/charges |
| Low quality certifications % | ↑ Risk of further recalls |

**Financial statement adjustments:**

| Metric | Peer Group | Well (Pre-ESG) | Well (Post-ESG) |
|--------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| COGS / Revenue | 13% | 13.3% | **14.5%** (+1.3%) |
| Non-op expenses / Op income | 3% | 3% | **7.5%** (+4.5%) |
| Revenue | — | Baseline | **–2%** (quality controversies) |

**Valuation implication**: Poor social management → higher costs, lower revenues, legal liabilities → **downward adjustment** to equity and wider credit spreads.

> **Key lesson**: Social risks (product safety, employee practices, customer relations) have **direct, quantifiable income statement impacts** — they are not soft factors.

---

#### Example 5: Governance Factors — Bank Holding Company (Sumiyoshi)

**Material ESG factor**: Board composition quality + credit risk (NPLs as governance proxy)

**Governance scorecard vs. peers:**

| Factor | Peer Group | Sumiyoshi | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| CEO duality | Yes | Yes | ⚠️ Both negative |
| Independent chairperson | Yes | **No** | 🔴 Red flag |
| Board independence | 47% | **36%** | 🔴 Below peers |
| Gender diversity | 17% female | **7% female** | 🔴 Poor |
| Long tenure directors (>10yr) | 0% | **14%** | 🔴 Red flag |
| Board committees | 5 (incl. governance) | **4** | 🔴 Missing governance committee |
| Directors with industry experience | 67% | **20%** | 🔴 Far below peers |
| Say-on-pay | Yes | **No** | 🔴 Red flag |
| Dual-class shares | No | No | ✅ Both fine |
| Straight voting | Yes | Yes | ✅ Both fine |

**Sumiyoshi lags peers across nearly every governance dimension.**

**Additional proxy for bank governance risk**: **Non-performing loans (NPLs)** — loans not current on interest/principal. Higher NPLs signal poor risk management and credit governance.

**Valuation implication**: Governance deficiencies → higher perceived risk → **upward adjustment to discount rate or credit spread**.

### Comparative Summary Across All Three Examples

| Example | ESG Pillar | Key Metric | Adjustment Direction | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|-----------|--------------------|-|
| Frizzle (Beverages) | Environmental | Water intensity | ✅ Favorable | ↓ COGS, ↑ gross margin |
| Well Pharma | Social | Product quality/recalls | 🔴 Unfavorable | ↓ Revenue, ↑ COGS, ↑ non-op expenses |
| Sumiyoshi (Bank) | Governance | Board quality + NPLs | 🔴 Unfavorable | ↑ Discount rate / credit spread |

### Exam Tips

- ESG integration = **quantified adjustments** to financials and valuation — not just qualitative commentary
- **Environmental** strength → cost savings → margin improvement (e.g. resource efficiency)
- **Social** weakness → revenue loss + cost increase + legal liabilities
- **Governance** weakness → higher cost of capital (discount rate ↑) or wider credit spreads
- For banks specifically, **NPLs** serve as a quantitative proxy for governance/risk management quality
- Whether ESG impacts are **recurring vs. non-recurring** matters for how adjustments are modeled
