### What is ESG?

ESG stands for:
- **E**nvironmental
- **S**ocial  
- **G**overnance

These three factors measure a company's sustainability and ethical impact beyond traditional financial metrics.

### Why ESG Matters

1. **Risk Management**: ESG factors can signal future financial risks (e.g., environmental liability, labor disputes)
2. **Long-term Value**: Companies with strong ESG practices often have better long-term returns
3. **Regulatory Trends**: Governments increasingly mandate ESG disclosure and compliance
4. **Investor Demand**: Institutional investors and funds increasingly screen for ESG criteria

### The Three Pillars

#### Environmental (E)
- Climate change risk and carbon emissions
- Resource efficiency and waste management
- Environmental compliance and violations
- Examples: BP oil spill, Tesla's renewable focus

#### Social (S)
- Labor practices and human rights
- Product safety and quality
- Community relations
- Examples: Apple's supply chain labor issues, Costco's employee treatment

#### Governance (G)
- Board composition and diversity
- Executive compensation
- Shareholder rights and protections
- Audit committee independence
- Examples: Enron scandal, Wells Fargo governance issues

### Integration into Investment Analysis

ESG factors should be:
- **Integrated** into fundamental analysis, not treated separately
- **Weighted** based on industry-specific materiality
- **Monitored** for changes in company practices and industry standards
- **Compared** to peer companies in the same sector

### ESG Scores vs. Fundamental Analysis

- **ESG ratings** are **not** substitutes for fundamental financial analysis
- Used as **complementary** indicators of risk and sustainability
- Different ESG rating agencies may produce different scores
- Focus on **material** ESG factors relevant to the company's industry
