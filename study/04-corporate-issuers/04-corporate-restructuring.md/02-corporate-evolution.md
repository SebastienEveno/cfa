### The Corporate Life Cycle

Companies progress through four stages, each with a different financing mix and strategic posture:

```
Start-up → Growth → Maturity → Decline
(negative FCF) → (FCF builds) → (peak FCF) → (FCF falls)
```

Managers take structural actions to **extend or exit** this cycle rather than simply letting it run to liquidation.

---

### Three Categories of Corporate Structural Change

| Category | Effect | Example |
|----------|--------|---------|
| **Investment** | ↑ Size / scope | Acquisition |
| **Divestment** | ↓ Size / scope | Sale or spin-off of a segment |
| **Restructuring** | Same size, improved structure | Cost cuts, balance sheet changes, reorganization |

---

### Top-Down Drivers (Macro/Industry)

**1. Pro-cyclicality of transactions**
- Corporate transactions strongly correlated with equity prices (BCG: correlation = 0.80, 2000–2019)
- Three explanations:
  - **Higher CEO confidence** during bull markets
  - **Lower cost of financing** (lower rates + lower ERP)
  - **Overvalued stock** can be used as acquisition currency

> **Counter-intuitive exam point**: "Weak economy" deals generate ~10% **higher** 3-year shareholder returns than "strong economy" deals — distress creates opportunity.

**2. Industry shocks**
- Regulatory changes, technological disruption, or industry growth changes trigger **restructuring waves**
- Companies adapt to competitive disruption

### Investment Actions

#### Three Types:

| Type | Control | Financial Treatment |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| **Equity investment** | <50% stake; no full control | Equity method accounting |
| **Joint venture** | Jointly controlled new entity | Equity method accounting |
| **Acquisition** | >50%; full control | Full consolidation of financials |

#### Key Motivations:

| Motivation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Cost synergies** | Economies of scale — consolidate HQ, reduce redundant functions, optimize manufacturing/distribution |
| **Revenue synergies** | Cross-selling, increased bargaining power, economies of scope |
| **Growth** | Acquire faster-growing companies; expand geographically |
| **Vertical integration** | Acquire suppliers or distributors → lower costs, reduced risk |
| **Undervalued target** | Opportunistic value creation |

#### Why Acquisitions Fail (~2/3 destroy value):
1. **Overpaying** → negative NPV even if business performs well
2. **Synergy overestimation** → unrealistic assumptions embedded in price
3. **Integration failure** → business process conflicts, talent loss, management replacement

### Divestment Actions

#### Two Types:

| Type | Mechanism | Seller Receives | Post-Transaction |
|------|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Sale (divestiture)** | Sells to acquirer for cash/stock | Cash (or stock) | No further exposure |
| **Spin-off** | Distributes shares of new co. to existing shareholders | No cash | Two independent companies |

#### Key Motivations:

| Motivation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Focus** | Separate incompatible or distinct businesses (e.g. Daimler → Mercedes-Benz + Daimler Trucks) |
| **Valuation / conglomerate discount** | Parts worth more separately (e.g. Novartis/Alcon: spin-off P/E was 2× parent) |
| **Liquidity** | Sell assets to reduce unsustainable leverage |
| **Regulatory** | Forced divestiture as condition of merger approval or antitrust remedy |

#### Sale vs. Spin-Off:

| Factor | Favors Sale | Favors Spin-Off |
|--------|------------|----------------|
| Liquidity needed | ✅ | ❌ |
| Multiple interested buyers | ✅ | |
| Higher independent valuation expected | | ✅ |
| Regulatory scrutiny concern | | ✅ (spin-offs rarely face scrutiny) |

**Conglomerate discount**: A company trading below the sum of its parts → divestment or spin-off can unlock value.

### Restructuring Actions

#### Three Types:

**1. Cost Restructuring**
- Goal: reduce costs, improve margins, raise returns to peer/historical levels
- Common triggers: underperformance, post-acquisition integration, activist threat
- Methods:
  - **Outsourcing**: subcontract standardizable processes (IT, HR, legal, manufacturing) to specialists with economies of scale
  - **Offshoring**: relocate operations to lower-cost countries (often combined with outsourcing)

**2. Balance Sheet Restructuring**
- Goal: alter asset/liability composition to improve WACC or liquidity

| Transaction | Mechanism | Primary Use |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| **Sale-leaseback** | Sell asset → immediately lease it back | Raise liquidity quickly; retain asset use |
| **Dividend recapitalization** | Issue debt → pay dividend or repurchase shares | Replace expensive equity with cheaper debt |

> **Sale-leaseback trade-off**: Cash upfront + reduced ownership risk vs. higher ongoing lease costs (lessor earns interest) + increased liabilities

> **Dividend recap**: ↓ WACC if debt < equity cost; ↑ financial leverage — only suitable for stable cash flow businesses

**3. Reorganization (Bankruptcy)**
- Court-supervised process for insolvent companies
- Business continues operating; existing management stays
- Creditors negotiate: debt-to-equity conversions, asset sales, refinancing
- **Outcome**: lighter debt burden; equity holders often wiped out
- **vs. Liquidation**: liquidation = reorganization failed; court sells assets and distributes proceeds to creditors per legal priority

### Leveraged Buyouts (LBOs)

A special case combining **investment + restructuring + eventual divestment**.

**Structure**: Private equity acquirer uses significant debt to acquire a target → restructures for value → exits via IPO or sale.

**Returns driven by four variables:**
1. **Purchase price** (lower = better)
2. **Amount of leverage** (amplifies returns)
3. **FCF generated** during ownership (used to pay down debt)
4. **Exit price** (higher = better)

**Hilton Hotels LBO (Blackstone, 2007–2018):**
- Acquired for $26B ($14.5B debt + $5.5B equity)
- Blackstone replaced management, pursued franchising strategy, divested flagship properties
- Re-listed 2013; Blackstone gradually sold 2013–2018
- **Net profit: $11B+ on $5.5B equity investment** over 11 years

**Cost synergy as % of Alexion standalone OpEx**:

**Post-acquisition impact on AstraZeneca:**

| Metric | Pre-Acquisition | Post-Acquisition | Change |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|--------|
| Revenue growth (Yr 2) | 10% | 12% | +200 bps |
| Revenue growth (Yr 3) | 9% | 11% | +200 bps |

### Exam Tips

- **2/3 of acquisitions fail** — know the three reasons: overpaying, synergy overestimation, integration
- **Spin-offs** = no cash to parent; **sales** = cash to parent — key distinction
- **Conglomerate discount** = sum of parts > whole → motivation for divestment
- **Sale-leaseback**: asset leaves balance sheet, lease liability appears, annual cost rises
- **Dividend recap**: only appropriate for **stable cash flow** businesses
- **Reorganization ≠ Liquidation**: reorganization = restructure debt and continue; liquidation = sell assets and distribute
- **Weak economy deals outperform** strong economy deals — counterintuitive but testable
- **LBO returns** = function of entry price, leverage, FCF, and exit price
