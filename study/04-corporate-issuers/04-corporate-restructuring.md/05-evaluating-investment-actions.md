| Case | Type | Key Theme |
|------|------|----------|
| Dilmun / Spina | Equity investment | Minority stake in growth company; ESG pressure |

## Case 1: Equity Investment — Dilmun Inc. & Spina Ltd.

### Context
- Dilmun = mature tobacco company; declining volumes, ESG pressure, strong margins (35%+ EBIT)
- Spina = fast-growing e-vapor market leader; deeply loss-making
- Deal: Dilmun buys **30% stake in Spina for $1.2B** → valued at $4B EV
- Financed: **debt at 600 bps**; maintains investment-grade rating
- Service agreement: Spina gets access to Dilmun's 225,000 retail locations

### Motivations

| Party | Motivation |
|-------|-----------|
| **Dilmun** | Diversify into adjacent growth market; investment exposure; improve ESG profile |
| **Spina** | Cash for product development; access to Dilmun's distribution without losing independence |

### Valuation Check — EV/Sales Multiples

| Company | EV/Sales |
|---------|---------|
| Comparable A | 4.5× |
| Comparable B | 10.0× |
| Comparable C | 5.1× |
| Comparable D | 4.9× |
| Comparable E | 6.0× |
| **Median** | **5.1×** |
| **Spina implied** | **6.7×** (4,000/600) |

→ Dilmun paid a **premium to peer median** — justified by Spina's market leadership and distribution synergies

### Pro Forma EPS & Leverage Impact on Dilmun (20X4E)

**Two channels of impact:**
1. **Income from associates**: Dilmun's 30% share of Spina's loss + amortization
   - = –(0.30 × $450M) – $10M = **–$145M**
2. **Interest expense**: $1.2B × 6% = **–$72M** (pre-tax)

| Metric | Before | After |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Net income | $7,256M | $7,084M |
| Diluted EPS | $3.94 | **$3.85** (↓$0.09) |
| Debt | $13,894M | $15,094M |
| Debt/EBITDA | 1.37× | **1.51×** |

> **Key lesson**: Equity investments in loss-making companies are **EPS dilutive** via equity method loss recognition, even without consolidation. Debt-financed → also increases leverage.

### Alternative Structures vs. Equity Investment

| Alternative | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Acquisition** | Full control; prevents Spina from acting against Dilmun's interests | Much larger capital outlay; higher risk |
| **Joint venture** | Governance representation; shared risk | Larger investment; may compromise Spina's independence |

## Case 2: Joint Venture — Opone SA / Hapalla AG (OHAA)

### Context
- OHAA = JV formed 20X1; Opone (Brazil) + Hapalla (Europe) each own 50%
- OHAA manufactures Hapalla-branded vehicles in Latin America
- OHAA grown from <10K to 1.5M annual vehicle sales (20X1–20X7)
- Both companies account for OHAA using **equity method** (50% share of profit)

### Mutual Benefit
- **Opone**: JV income > 100% of net income — core operations run at a net loss
- **Hapalla**: Geographic expansion into Latin America with established local partner; shared risk

### Valuation Anomaly — P/E vs. P/FCF

| Metric | Opone SA | Peer Median |
|--------|---------|------------|
| P/E | **21×** (expensive) | 12× |
| P/FCF | **7×** (cheap) | 17× |

**Why the disconnect?** In 20X7, OHAA paid BRL40B in dividends but earned only BRL15.3B in profit → OHAA was distributing **cash far in excess of earnings** (running down its cash balance). Opone only recognizes its 50% share of **profit** as income, but receives 50% of **cash dividends** as FCF. FCF is much higher than income → P/FCF appears cheap.

### Hapalla's Partial Buyout Offer

**Offer**: BRL 45B for additional 25% stake (→ Hapalla goes from 50% to 75%)

**Implied OHAA valuation**:
$$EV = \frac{45B}{0.25} = BRL\ 180B$$
$$\text{Equity value} = 180B - 32.5B\ \text{cash} = BRL\ 147.5B$$
$$P/E = \frac{147.5B}{15.3B} = \mathbf{9.6\times}\ (\text{vs. peer median 12}\times)$$

→ Hapalla is acquiring at a **discount to peers** — favorable for Hapalla

### Pro Forma for Opone SA (if it accepts)

**Two effects:**
1. De-recognize 25% interest (BRL 13B) → **gain = 45B − 13B = BRL 32B**
2. JV income falls from 50% to 25% of OHAA profits → **−BRL 4,689M**

| Item | Before | After |
|------|--------|-------|
| JV income | 9,379 | 4,689 |
| Gain on sale | 0 | **32,000** |
| Net income | 7,761 | **31,735** |

### Effect on Hapalla AG

- Changes from **equity method → full consolidation** of OHAA
- JV income line disappears; full revenues/expenses consolidated
- Non-controlling interest (Opone's 25%) recognized on balance sheet
- Cash reduced by BRL 45B paid to Opone

> **Critical exam concept**: Crossing the **50% ownership threshold** → accounting switches from equity method to consolidation — dramatically changes all financial statement line items.

## Case 3: Acquisition — Tulor / Caracol Retail Segment

### Context
- Tulor = Australian convenience store operator
- Caracol = global oil & gas company; selling Retail segment to reduce high leverage
- Deal: AUD 3B total (AUD 2B cash + 80M Tulor shares)
- Caracol uses after-tax cash (AUD 1.6B) entirely for debt retirement
- Caracol receives 80M Tulor shares → locked up for 5 years

### Motivations

| Party | Motivation |
|-------|-----------|
| **Tulor** | Synergies ($125M EBITDA by Year 3) + scale/growth in mature industry |
| **Caracol** | Balance sheet repair (retire debt) + focus on core Upstream/Downstream |

### Valuation Check

| | Multiple |
|--|---------|
| Peer median EV/EBITDA | **8×** |
| Acquisition EV/EBITDA (20X2 EBITDA) | **16×** (= 3,000/183) |
| Acquisition EV/EBITDA (incl. $125M synergies) | **10×** (= 3,000/308) |

→ Raw multiple appears high (16× vs. 8× peers) — **two key reasons:**
1. **Control premium**: trading multiples reflect minority stakes; acquisitions include full control
2. **Synergies**: adjusting for AUD 125M in expected synergies → multiple drops to **10×**, within peer range

> **Exam lesson**: Always adjust acquisition multiples for synergies when benchmarking vs. trading comps.

### Pro Forma EPS & Leverage Impact on Tulor (20X3)

**Key adjustments:**
- +AUD 232M EBITDA from Retail (including AUD 42M synergies)
- +AUD 200M incremental amortization of acquired intangibles
- +AUD 60M new interest expense (on AUD 1B new debt)
- +80M new shares issued

| Metric | Before | After |
|--------|--------|-------|
| EBITDA | 1,803 | **2,035** ↑ |
| Net income | 620 | **506** ↓ |
| Diluted EPS | $0.96 | **$0.70** ↓ |
| Shares outstanding | 648M | **728M** |
| Debt/EBITDA | 3.4× | **3.5×** |

> **Key insight**: Acquisition is **EPS dilutive** despite EBITDA accretion — driven by:
> - **$200M amortization** of acquired intangibles (large non-cash drag)
> - **80M new shares** issued (denominator increase)
> - Partially offset by synergies and tax effects

### Master Comparison: Three Investment Types

| Feature | Equity Investment | Joint Venture | Acquisition |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|-------------|
| Control | ❌ None | Partial | ✅ Full |
| Accounting | Equity method | Equity method | Consolidation |
| Income recognition | % of investee profit | % of JV profit | Full consolidation |
| Capital required | Lowest | Medium | Highest |
| Financial statement impact | Equity income line only | Equity income line only | All lines combined |
| EPS impact | Usually dilutive if target unprofitable | Depends on JV profitability | Often dilutive (intangibles amortization + shares) |
| Leverage impact | ↑ if debt-financed | ↑ if debt-financed | ↑ significantly |

---

### Exam Tips

- **Equity method**: recognize % of investee **profit/loss** — not revenue/expenses
- **Loss-making investee** + debt-financed = **doubly dilutive** to EPS
- **JV → consolidation** trigger: crossing 50% ownership → dramatic financial statement change
- **Dividends from JV > JV profits**: FCF > income → P/FCF appears lower than P/E (Opone case)
- **Acquisition EV/EBITDA**: always check both raw multiple and **synergy-adjusted multiple**
- **Amortization of acquired intangibles** is often the biggest EPS drag in acquisitions
- **Equity-financed acquisitions** → share count ↑ → EPS dilution from denominator alone
- **Seller receives equity** in acquirer → creates a new equity investment relationship (Caracol/Tulor)
