Two detailed case studies: a **cost restructuring** (Cyrene) and a **balance sheet restructuring via sale-leaseback** (Kosala).

---

## Case 1: Cost Restructuring — Cyrene SARL

### Context
- Cyrene = European consumer goods company; **below-peer EBIT margins** (14% vs. peer median 20%)
- Trigger: **Rejected unsolicited acquisition offer** at 20% premium → shareholders pressure management to self-improve or remain vulnerable to another bid
- Response: Comprehensive cost structure review

### Key Message from Shareholders
> "For as long as you don't take actions to increase shareholder value, you are vulnerable to an acquirer who will."

Meaning: Cyrene's **below-average profitability** makes it an attractive LBO/acquisition target — an acquirer could buy it, cut costs, and earn an attractive return. Cyrene must close the margin gap independently.

---

### Profitability Gap Analysis

**Current vs. peer median EBIT margin:**

| Metric | Cyrene | Peer Median |
|--------|--------|------------|
| EBIT margin | **14%** | **20%** |
| Revenue (LTM) | €18,990M | — |
| EBIT | €2,659M | — |
| Implied OpEx | €16,331M | — |

**Required cost reduction to reach peer median:**
$$\text{Target EBIT} = 18,990 \times 20\% = €3,798M$$
$$\text{Required reduction} = 3,798 - 2,659 = \mathbf{€1,139M\ (7\%\ of\ OpEx)}$$

---

### Pro Forma Next 12 Months (NTM) Estimates

**Assumptions:**
- Revenue growth: 3%/yr
- EBIT margin improves evenly: (20% − 14%) / 4 = **+1.5% per year**
- One-time restructuring costs: €1,250M

| Metric | LTM | NTM |
|--------|-----|-----|
| Revenue | €18,990M | €19,560M |
| EBIT (ex. restructuring) | €2,659M | €3,032M (15.5% margin) |
| Restructuring costs | — | €1,250M |
| **Pro forma EBIT** | — | **€1,782M (9.1% margin)** |

> **Key insight**: Year 1 EBIT is **lower** than pre-restructuring because one-time costs swamp the margin improvement. Value is realized over Years 2–4.

---

### Risks of Cost Restructuring

| Risk | Explanation |
|------|------------|
| **Revenue deceleration** | Cyrene grew 4% vs. peer average ~1% — cutting costs may eliminate the spending driving that growth advantage |
| **Political/regulatory risk** | Layoffs and facility closures → government pressure, public backlash; consumer-facing company especially vulnerable |

---

### Alternative: Sell/Spin-Off Household Goods Segment

**Segment EBIT margins:**

| Segment | Revenue | EBIT | Margin | Growth |
|---------|---------|------|--------|--------|
| Household Goods | €5,507M | €496M | 9% | 7% |
| Beauty & Personal Care | €8,166M | €1,960M | 24% | 3% |
| Food | €5,317M | €583M | 11% | 2% |
| Corporate | — | (€380M) | — | — |

**Pro forma after removing Household Goods:**

| Metric | Before | After Removal |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| Revenue | €18,990M | €13,483M |
| EBIT | €2,659M | €2,163M |
| EBIT margin | **14%** | **16%** |

**Trade-off**: Margin improves by 2 percentage points but **total EBIT falls 19%** — and Household Goods is the **fastest-growing** segment (7% vs. 2–3% for others).

**Additional analysis needed:**
- Valuation of Household Goods in sale/spin-off vs. implied current value
- Synergies/dis-synergies with remaining segments
- Corporate cost reduction potential upon separation
- Whether both restructuring AND divestment could be pursued simultaneously

## Case 2: Balance Sheet Restructuring — Kosala Corp. Sale-Leaseback

### Context
- Kosala = global omnichannel retailer; **owns distribution center real estate** (non-core asset)
- Sale-leaseback of distribution centers → receive cash → retire debt + repurchase shares
- Key motivation: currently **speculative-grade rated** → improvement to **investment-grade** would lower cost of capital

### Transaction Terms
| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Cash proceeds | CHF 425M |
| Debt retired | CHF 215M |
| Share repurchase | 10M shares |
| Annual lease expense | CHF 19M/yr |
| Cap rate | **4.5%** |
| Lease term | 15 years |

### Cap Rate Evaluation

**Lower cap rate = higher valuation for seller:**
| Percentile | Cap Rate |
|-----------|---------|
| 25th | 3.0% |
| **50th (median)** | **5.5%** |
| 75th | 8.0% |
| **Kosala's deal** | **4.5%** ✅ |

→ Kosala achieved a **below-median cap rate** (above-median valuation) — favorable to the seller.

**Factors influencing cap rate**: property location (metropolitan proximity) and physical condition.

---

### Pro Forma Financial Impact

**Key adjustments:**
- ↑ SG&A by CHF 19M (lease expense)
- ↓ D&A by CHF 30M (no longer owns assets)
- + Amortization of gain on sale: CHF 200M / 15 years = CHF 13M/yr → D&A net change = −30 + 13 = **−17M**
- ↓ Interest expense by CHF 15M (debt retired)
- ↓ Debt by CHF 215M
- ↓ Shares by 10M (repurchase)

| Metric | Pre-Transaction | Post-Transaction |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| EBITDA | 191M | 191M (approx.) |
| Gross debt | CHF 615M | **CHF 400M** |
| **Debt/EBITDA** | **3.2×** | **2.3×** |
| EBIT/Interest | **2.9×** | **4.3×** |
| Diluted EPS | CHF 0.63 | **CHF 0.81** |
| Shares outstanding | 97M | **87M** |

---

### Credit Rating Upgrade Calculation

Using credit rating/spread table:

| Rating | Debt/EBITDA | EBIT/Interest | Spread over Treasury |
|--------|------------|--------------|---------------------|
| AAA/AA | 0–1.0× | >12× | +125 bps |
| A | 1.0–1.5× | 8–11× | +232 bps |
| **BBB** | **1.6–2.3×** | **4.0–7.9×** | **+450 bps** |
| BB | 2.4–3.5× | 1.6–3.5× | +575 bps |
| B | 3.6–4.5× | 0.5–1.5× | +731 bps |

**Kosala's pro forma ratios** (2.3× and 4.3×) → **BBB rating** → spread = 450 bps

$$r_d = 1.25\% + 4.50\% = 5.75\%$$
$$\text{Pro forma interest} = 400M \times 5.75\% = CHF\ 23M\ \text{vs. CHF 28M previously}$$

→ Credit rating upgrade provides **additional CHF 5M annual interest savings** beyond the debt reduction itself.

### Master Framework for Restructuring Evaluation

#### Cost Restructuring:
```
1. Calculate margin gap vs. peer median
2. Estimate required cost reduction (€ and % of OpEx)
3. Model annual pace of improvement (even or front-loaded)
4. Add one-time restructuring costs in Year 1
5. Assess risks: revenue impact, political/regulatory
6. Compare vs. alternative: divestment of underperforming segment
```

#### Balance Sheet Restructuring (Sale-Leaseback):
```
1. Identify motivation (liquidity, credit rating, unlock non-core asset value)
2. Evaluate cap rate vs. comparable transactions (lower = better for seller)
3. Build pro forma: +lease expense, -D&A, -interest, -debt, -shares
4. Calculate new Debt/EBITDA and Interest Coverage
5. Map to credit rating table → estimate new spread → new interest expense
6. Quantify total benefit: EPS, leverage, WACC
```

---

### Key Exam Formulas

$$\text{Target OpEx reduction} = \text{Revenue} \times (\text{Target margin} - \text{Current margin})$$

$$\text{Annual margin improvement} = \frac{\text{Target margin} - \text{Current margin}}{\text{Years to achieve}}$$

$$\text{Cap rate} = \frac{\text{Net operating income}}{\text{Property value}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{Value} = \frac{\text{NOI}}{\text{Cap rate}}$$

$$\text{Pro forma interest} = \text{New debt} \times (r_f + \text{Credit spread at new rating})$$

---

### Exam Tips

- **Cost restructuring**: Year 1 EPS often **worsens** due to one-time costs — value realized in Years 2+
- **Divestment of low-margin segment**: improves margin but **reduces total EBIT** — not automatically better
- **Sale-leaseback**: asset leaves balance sheet → D&A ↓; lease expense ↑; net P&L impact depends on relative magnitudes
- **Cap rate**: **lower cap rate = higher valuation** for the seller — always check vs. comparable transactions
- **Credit rating improvement**: produces **double benefit** — lower debt level AND lower interest rate
- **BBB→BB cliff**: avoid at all costs — WACC jumps dramatically; most investment-grade companies structure transactions to stay above this threshold
- Always check whether **EPS is accretive or dilutive** after restructuring — sale-leaseback can be EPS accretive if interest savings + share repurchase > incremental lease cost
